SUR AMERICA — SURVEY SHEET

Geographic position

The Spine of South America

The Andes form the entire western boundary of South America — a near-vertical wall from Venezuela to Tierra del Fuego. The western coast follows the mountains so closely that the range and the Pacific shore are inseparable.

HYDROGRAPHIC SURVEY — GLACIAL SYSTEMS

Glaciers and water resources

Ice and the Water Supply

The Andes function as a continental water tower — their glaciers and snowfields supply freshwater to cities and agriculture across the Pacific coast and beyond.

~1,800 glaciers
Tropical glaciers
Over 99% of all tropical glaciers on Earth are in the Andes. Peru alone holds 71% of the world's tropical glacier area.
30%
Freshwater supply share
Andean glaciers and snowfields supply nearly a third of South America's freshwater, feeding the coastal river systems that sustain Pacific-slope cities.
17,200 km²
S. Patagonian Ice Field
Largest temperate ice mass in the Southern Hemisphere. Feeds 48 major glaciers, including Perito Moreno — one of the few in the world still advancing.
40%
Area lost 1980–2020
Tropical Andean glaciers have retreated by approximately 40% in 40 years. Current projections show near-complete loss of smaller glaciers by 2100.

CULTURAL SURVEY — ANCIENT INFRASTRUCTURE

Ancient infrastructure of the Andes

The Inca — Builders of the Mountain World

The Inca Empire was the most sophisticated state ever built in the Americas — and it was constructed vertically, at altitude, in direct relationship with the Andes.

Mountains as Deities — Apus
Every major peak was an Apu — a divine spirit governing weather, harvests, and fate. Highest peaks were sites of the capacocha ritual. Mummies at 6,739 m on Llullaillaco remain the highest archaeological site on Earth.
6,739 m — highest archaeological site
Qhapaq Ñan — Road Network
40,000 km of roads, more extensive than Rome's network. Stone-paved mountain highways, suspension bridges over canyon gorges, and relay runners covering 240 km/day. UNESCO World Heritage, crossing six countries.
40,000 km across 6 modern nations
Andenes — Terrace Agriculture
1 million+ hectares of agricultural terraces, engineered with integrated drainage, irrigation, and frost management. Different crops cultivated at controlled altitudes. Many terraces remain active today.
1,000,000+ ha of terraces
Machu Picchu — 2,430 m
Royal estate, agricultural center, and astronomical observatory built c. 1450 CE. Aligned to solstice sunrises and mountain peaks. Hidden from the outside world for 400 years. Now the world's most visited archaeological site.
Built c. 1450 CE
Empire and Legacy
Peak extent: 4,000 km along the Andes, population ~12 million. Spanish conquest 1532–1572. Legacy: ~10 million Quechua speakers; active terracing communities; Apu ceremony tradition unbroken.
~10M Quechua speakers, 2024

ASTRONOMICAL SURVEY — ATACAMA PLATEAU, CHILE

Astronomical observatories

The World's Premier Observatory Site

The Atacama Plateau, 4,000–5,000 m above sea level at the Andean western escarpment, provides conditions unmatched anywhere on Earth for astronomical observation.

5,058 m a.s.l.
ALMA Observatory
66 high-precision antennas, Chajnantor Plateau. The most powerful millimeter/submillimeter telescope array ever built. Primary mission: cold universe — star-forming regions, planetary disks, distant galaxies.
Site Conditions
Atacama Advantage
<1 mm precipitable water vapor. 330+ cloudless nights/year. Elevation: 5,000 m (above 50% of atmosphere). Zero light pollution radius: 200+ km. Considered by astronomers as Earth's closest analog to outer space.
2,635 m a.s.l.
Very Large Telescope
Four 8.2-m telescopes on Cerro Paranal, Chile. First direct exoplanet image captured here (2004). Deepest optical survey images ever produced. Part of the European Southern Observatory.
Under Construction
Extremely Large Telescope
Cerro Armazones, 3,060 m, Chile. 39-metre primary mirror. Expected completion: 2028. Will be the largest optical-infrared telescope on Earth. Target: first light from galaxies, exoplanet atmospheres.
