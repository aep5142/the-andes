ALMA — Atacama Large Millimeter Array

66 Telescopes Acting as One

At 5,058 meters above sea level, ALMA is the highest major astronomical observatory on Earth. Sixty-six antennas work together as a single instrument — the largest in the world — listening to the universe in wavelengths invisible to human eyes. It watches the birth of planets, the spin of distant galaxies, the chemistry of the first stars.

The Atacama plateau is ideal: no rain, no vegetation, minimal atmosphere between the antennas and the sky. On some days, the relative humidity registers zero.

Why here

What Makes the Atacama Extraordinary

Astronomy requires the sky to cooperate. The Atacama offers conditions found nowhere else on Earth — a combination of altitude, aridity, and isolation that no other site matches.

↑
Altitude
5,000 m above sea level
At 5,000 m, you are above 50% of Earth's atmosphere. Less air means less absorption, less turbulence, less everything that blurs starlight. Gamma rays, millimeter waves, and infrared that never reach sea level arrive here intact.
○
Aridity
< 1 mm rain per year (some regions)
Water vapor is the astronomer's enemy — it absorbs infrared and radio waves. The Atacama is the driest non-polar desert on Earth. Some weather stations have never recorded rainfall in their operational history.
✦
Darkness
330+ clear nights per year
The Atacama sees cloud cover on fewer than 35 nights per year. The nearest cities are hundreds of kilometers away. From the plateau, the Milky Way casts shadows on cloudless nights.
◎
Southern Sky
27°S latitude
From the southern hemisphere, telescopes have a direct view of the galactic center — the densest, most active region of the Milky Way. The Large and Small Magellanic Clouds, our nearest galactic neighbors, are visible to the naked eye.

The instruments

The Observatories of the Atacama

No other area on Earth concentrates as much astronomical instrumentation. The European Southern Observatory alone operates three major sites within 100 km of each other on this plateau.

ALMA
Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array
5,058 m — Llano de Chajnantor
A joint project of Europe, North America, and East Asia. ALMA's 66 antennas can be spread up to 16 km apart, giving it the resolution of a 16 km telescope. It watches cold matter — dust, gas, molecules — the raw material of stars and planets.
Discovered: first dust ring around a star, protoplanetary systems in formation, molecular complexity in space
VLT
Very Large Telescope — ESO
2,635 m — Cerro Paranal
Four 8.2-meter telescopes that can combine their light into a single instrument — effectively creating the largest optical telescope on Earth. Each mirror was polished to within 10 nanometers of perfection over a period of years.
Captured first direct image of an exoplanet. Confirmed the accelerating expansion of the universe.
ELT
Extremely Large Telescope — Under construction
3,046 m — Cerro Armazones
When completed, the ELT will be the largest optical/infrared telescope in the world — a 39-meter mirror made of 798 hexagonal segments. It will be able to directly image Earth-sized planets around nearby stars and analyze their atmospheres for signs of life.
Goal: detect biosignatures in exoplanet atmospheres. First light expected 2028.
Gemini South
Gemini South Observatory — NOIRLab
2,722 m — Cerro Pachón
One of two 8.1-meter Gemini telescopes on opposite sides of the Earth — Gemini North in Hawaii, Gemini South here. Together they provide full-sky coverage to astronomers from partner nations including the United States, Canada, Argentina, Chile, and Brazil.
First image of a multiple-planet exoplanet system.

What we learned

Discoveries from the Sky Above the Andes

The instruments on the Atacama plateau have fundamentally changed our picture of the universe — from the structure of galaxies to the possibility of life on other worlds.

1998
The universe is accelerating
Observations from Cerro Tololo Inter-American Observatory (part of the Atacama complex) contributed to the discovery that the expansion of the universe is speeding up — implying the existence of dark energy. This won the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics.
2004
First direct image of an exoplanet
The VLT captured the first confirmed direct image of a planet outside our solar system: 2M1207b, a planet 5 times the mass of Jupiter. Before this, all exoplanet detections had been indirect — measuring stellar wobbles or dimming.
2014
Planet formation in real time
ALMA captured the first detailed image of protoplanetary disk HL Tau — a young star surrounded by rings of dust and gas in the process of forming planets. The image redefined our understanding of how quickly planets form.
2019
The first black hole image
The Event Horizon Telescope — a global network that included ALMA as its most sensitive node — captured the first image of a black hole's shadow: M87*, 6.5 billion times the mass of the Sun, 55 million light years away.
