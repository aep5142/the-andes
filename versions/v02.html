Elevation landmarks

The Highest Points

Five of the six highest peaks outside Asia rise from the Andes. The range holds an unbroken wall of summits above 6,000 m — a scale found nowhere else outside the Himalayas.

6,962 m
Aconcagua
Mendoza, Argentina
Highest peak in the Western and Southern Hemispheres. The summit marks the Argentina–Chile border at 32°S.
6,893 m
Ojos del Salado
Argentina / Chile border
Earth's highest active volcano. Its crater lake — at ~6,390 m — is the world's highest lake by any measure.
6,768 m
Nevado Huascarán
Ancash, Peru
Highest point in Peru and in the tropical Andes. Its glaciers feed rivers that supply water to millions below.
6,542 m
Nevado Sajama
Oruro, Bolivia
Bolivia's highest peak, an extinct stratovolcano rising above the Altiplano. Sacred to the Aymara people.
6,310 m
Chimborazo
Chimborazo, Ecuador
Due to Earth's equatorial bulge, Chimborazo's summit is the point farthest from Earth's center — further than Everest.

How they were built

250 Million Years in the Making

The Andes rose to their current height largely in the last 25 million years, driven by the Nazca oceanic plate diving beneath South America at extraordinary speed.

~70 mm/yr
Rate of Nazca Plate subduction
One of Earth's fastest subduction rates. As the Pacific plate dives beneath South America, it compresses, thickens, and lifts the crust above.
70 km
Average crustal thickness
Among the thickest continental crust on Earth. The global average is 35 km. This extra mass is what sustains the mountains' extraordinary height.
200+
Active volcanoes
The subducting plate melts as it descends, creating a chain of active volcanoes — the most concentrated in the world — along the Pacific Ring of Fire.

Scale in context

Longer Than Any Range on Earth

Measured end-to-end, the Andes are nearly twice the length of the Himalayas and more than three times the Alps. No mountain range on Earth spans as many degrees of latitude.
