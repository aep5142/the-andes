Seven nations, one spine

The Countries the Andes Cross

From the Caribbean coast of Venezuela to the windswept tip of Patagonia, the Andes divide the continent — separating Pacific coastal deserts from Amazonian rainforests.

Venezuela
8–12°N — Northern terminus
The Mérida Andes form a northeastern branch here. Pico Bolívar (4,978 m) once held glaciers — they are now completely gone.
Highest: Pico Bolívar — 4,978 m
Colombia
0–8°N — Three cordilleras
Uniquely, the Andes split into three parallel ranges in Colombia. The valley between them holds Bogotá. Both Pacific and Caribbean slopes drain here.
Highest: Pico Cristóbal Colón — 5,775 m
Ecuador
0–4°S — The Avenue of Volcanoes
A double row of volcanoes straddles the equator. Chimborazo, Cotopaxi, and others form one of Earth's most dramatic volcanic landscapes.
Highest: Chimborazo — 6,310 m
Peru
0–18°S — Cradle of civilizations
The Andes split Peru between Amazon and coast. The Cordillera Blanca holds the world's largest tropical glaciers. The Amazon River begins here.
Highest: Nevado Huascarán — 6,768 m
Bolivia
14–22°S — The Altiplano
The widest part of the Andes. A high plateau at 3,800 m average elevation, ringed by peaks above 6,000 m. Lake Titicaca sits at its northern edge.
Highest: Nevado Sajama — 6,542 m
Chile & Argentina
22–55°S — Aconcagua to Patagonia
The border between Chile and Argentina follows the Andes for almost its entire length. Aconcagua — the hemisphere's highest peak — stands here, alongside the Patagonian ice fields.
Highest: Aconcagua — 6,962 m

North to south

A Traverse of the World

Walking the length of the Andes would pass through every climate zone on Earth — more ecological zones than any other linear path on the planet's surface.

11°N
Tropical Cloud Forest
The Andes begin in mist. Humid air from the Caribbean creates cloud forests at 2,000–3,500 m — biodiversity hotspots unlike anywhere else.
0°
Equatorial Volcanoes
At the equator, snow-capped volcanoes rise above the clouds. Snow and equatorial sun coexist. The Inca called the mountains Apus — divine spirits.
14°S
The Altiplano
The world's highest inhabited plateau. At 3,800 m, pink flamingos wade in turquoise salt lakes. Ancient cities like Tiwanaku flourished here for millennia.
24°S
The Atacama Desert
The driest non-polar desert on Earth sits in the Andes' rain shadow. Virtually no rainfall — yet rivers from glaciers sustain cities along the coast.
33°S
Aconcagua Zone
The highest point in the Americas. Bare, wind-blasted, almost extraterrestrial. The Andes at their tallest and most austere.
50°S
Patagonian Ice
The Andes end as they began — in ice. The Southern Patagonian Ice Field is the largest contiguous ice mass in the Southern Hemisphere outside Antarctica.
