04 — Hydrology

Ice that feeds a continent

The Water Tower of South America

The Andes hold the world's largest concentration of tropical glaciers and the Southern Hemisphere's second-largest ice mass outside Antarctica. Their meltwater is the foundation of life across the continent.

~1,800 glaciers
Tropical glaciers in the Andes
More than 99% of all tropical glaciers on Earth are in the Andes — concentrated in Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, and Colombia above 4,500 m.
30%
Of South America's freshwater
Andean glaciers and snowfields supply nearly a third of the continent's freshwater — sustaining cities, agriculture, and ecosystems across the Pacific coast and beyond.
17,200 km²
Southern Patagonian Ice Field
The largest temperate ice mass in the Southern Hemisphere. Its glaciers include Perito Moreno — one of the few in the world still advancing.
40%
Volume lost since 1980
Tropical Andean glaciers have lost roughly 40% of their area in 40 years. Cities like La Paz, Lima, and Quito face critical freshwater shortfalls as the ice retreats.
05 — Civilization

People of the high peaks

The Inca and the Mountains That Were Gods

For the Inca, every major peak was an Apu — a divine spirit with power over rain, frost, and human affairs. Their entire civilization was built in vertical relationship with the Andes.

The Mountain Gods — Apus
Every major peak was a living deity. The higher the mountain, the greater its power. The most sacred Inca ritual, the capacocha, brought offerings — sometimes human — to the highest reachable summits. Mummies found at 6,739 m on Llullaillaco remain the highest archaeological site on Earth.
6,739 m — Llullaillaco, site of the capacocha discovery
Qhapaq Ñan — The Royal Road
The Inca built 40,000 km of roads across the Andes — more extensive than the Roman road network. Stone-paved, with suspension bridges over canyons, relay stations every 20 km, and chasquis (runners) who could carry messages 240 km a day. A UNESCO World Heritage site spanning six countries.
40,000 km — total road network
Andenes — Terrace Agriculture
The Inca transformed vertical mountainsides into productive farmland through andenes — stone-walled terraces with built-in irrigation, frost protection, and soil management. Different crops grew at different altitudes: maize below, potatoes and quinoa above. Over 1 million hectares of terracing still exist across the Andes.
1,000,000+ hectares of terraced land
Machu Picchu — City in the Clouds
Built at 2,430 m on a ridge above the Urubamba River, Machu Picchu was simultaneously a royal estate, agricultural center, and astronomical observatory. Its alignments with solstice sunrises and mountain peaks show that the Inca read the cosmos through the mountains. Hidden from the outside world for 400 years after the Spanish conquest.
Built c. 1450 CE — elevation 2,430 m
Empire and Enduring Legacy
At its peak, the Inca Empire stretched 4,000 km along the Andes with a population of 12 million. The Spanish conquest of 1532 dismantled the empire but not its people. Today roughly 10 million people speak Quechua, the Inca language. Andean communities still name mountains as Apus, still farm terraces, still hold ceremonies on the peaks.
~10 million Quechua speakers today

07 — Astronomy

The sky above the Andes

Where Science Meets the Universe

The Atacama Plateau — 4,000–5,000 m above sea level, at the edge of the world's driest desert — offers conditions unmatched on Earth for observing the sky: near-zero humidity, 330+ clear nights per year, and air so thin the universe comes into sharp focus.

Location
ALMA Observatory
The Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array sits at 5,058 m on the Chajnantor Plateau, Chile. Its 66 high-precision antennas work in concert to form the most powerful radio telescope array ever built, studying the cold, dark universe — the birthplaces of stars and galaxies.
Conditions
Why the Atacama
Less than 1 mm of precipitable water vapor in the atmosphere. 330+ cloudless nights per year. At 5,000 m, half the atmosphere stands below the telescope. No light pollution for hundreds of kilometers. Astronomers call it the closest thing on Earth to outer space.
Discovery
The Birth of Planets
In 2014, ALMA captured the first detailed images of a protoplanetary disk — rings of gas and dust around the young star HL Tauri showing planets forming in real time. The observation overturned existing models of how planetary systems develop and formed.
History
5,000 Years of Andean Astronomy
The Inca read the sky with extraordinary precision — tracking the Pleiades for agricultural calendars, identifying "dark constellations" in the Milky Way's dust lanes, and aligning cities and temples to solstice and equinox positions. ALMA stands on ground that humans have used to study the stars for five millennia.
