The Spine of South America

05 — Hydrology

Ice that feeds a continent

The Water Tower of South America

The Andes hold the world's largest concentration of tropical glaciers and the Southern Hemisphere's second-largest ice mass outside Antarctica. Their meltwater is the foundation of life across the continent.

~1,800 glaciers
Tropical glaciers
Over 99% of all tropical glaciers on Earth are in the Andes — concentrated in Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, and Colombia above 4,500 m.
30%
Of South America's freshwater
Andean glaciers and snowfields supply nearly a third of the continent's freshwater — sustaining cities, agriculture, and ecosystems across the Pacific coast and beyond.
17,200 km²
Southern Patagonian Ice Field
The largest temperate ice mass in the Southern Hemisphere. Feeds 48 major glaciers including Perito Moreno — one of the few still advancing.
40%
Volume lost since 1980
Tropical Andean glaciers have lost roughly 40% of their area in 40 years. Cities like La Paz, Lima, and Quito face critical freshwater shortfalls as a result.
06 — Civilization

The people of the high peaks

The Inca and the Mountains That Were Gods

For the Inca, every major peak was an Apu — a divine spirit controlling rain, frost, harvests, and fate. Their entire civilization was built in vertical relationship with the Andes.

The Mountain Gods — Apus
Every major peak was a living deity. The sacred capacocha ritual brought offerings — sometimes human — to the highest reachable summits. Mummies at 6,739 m on Llullaillaco remain the highest archaeological site on Earth.
6,739 m — Llullaillaco capacocha
Qhapaq Ñan — The Royal Road
40,000 km of roads — more extensive than Rome's network. Stone-paved highways, suspension bridges over canyon gorges, and relay runners covering 240 km/day. UNESCO World Heritage across six nations.
40,000 km of roads
Andenes — Terrace Agriculture
The Inca transformed vertical mountainsides into productive farmland through stone terraces — with built-in irrigation, frost protection, and altitude-zoned crops. Over 1 million hectares remain across the Andes.
1,000,000+ hectares terraced
Machu Picchu — 2,430 m
Royal estate, agricultural center, and astronomical observatory simultaneously. Aligned to solstice sunrises and mountain peaks. Hidden from the outside world for 400 years after the Spanish conquest.
Built c. 1450 CE
Empire and Legacy
Peak extent: 4,000 km along the Andes, population ~12 million. Spanish conquest 1532. Today ~10 million Quechua speakers remain. Andean communities still name peaks as Apus, still hold ceremonies at high summits.
~10 million Quechua speakers today
07 — Astronomy

The sky above the Andes

Where Science Meets the Universe

The Atacama Plateau — 4,000–5,000 m above sea level, at the edge of the world's driest desert — offers conditions unmatched anywhere on Earth for observing the sky. Less than 1 mm of precipitable water vapor. 330+ clear nights per year. An atmosphere so thin the universe comes into sharp focus.

Location

ALMA Observatory

The Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array — 66 high-precision antennas on the Chajnantor Plateau at 5,058 m. The most powerful radio telescope array ever built. It studies the cold universe: the birthplaces of stars and planets, the structure of distant galaxies, the molecules of life in interstellar space.

Conditions
Why the Atacama
Less than 1 mm precipitable water vapor. 330+ cloudless nights per year. At 5,000 m, astronomers work above half the atmosphere. Astronomers call it the closest thing on Earth to outer space. No light pollution for hundreds of kilometers.
Discovery
The Birth of Planets
In 2014, ALMA captured the first images of a protoplanetary disk in extraordinary detail — rings of gas and dust around HL Tauri showing planets forming in real time. A single observation that overturned existing models of how solar systems form.
Observatory
Very Large Telescope
Four 8.2-metre telescopes on Cerro Paranal at 2,635 m. The first direct image of an exoplanet. The deepest optical surveys of the universe ever produced. Part of the European Southern Observatory network.
History
5,000 Years of Andean Astronomy
The Inca tracked the Pleiades for agricultural calendars, identified dark constellations in the Milky Way's dust lanes, and aligned cities to solstice positions. ALMA stands on ground that humans have used to study the stars for five millennia.
