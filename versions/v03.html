What is at stake

Ice That Feeds a Continent

Andean glaciers act as natural reservoirs — storing snowfall in winter, releasing meltwater in dry seasons. As they vanish, entire cities face water scarcity during the months they need it most.

1,800 glaciers
In Peru alone
Peru hosts the largest concentration of tropical glaciers in the world — yet it has lost more than 50% of its glacial coverage since the 1960s.
−0.93 m/yr
Annual ice loss rate
The Andean cryosphere is thinning at nearly one meter per year. Some glaciers — like Chacaltaya in Bolivia — have already disappeared entirely.
2030
Projected disappearance for small glaciers
Small glaciers below 5,400 m are expected to be gone by 2030 under current warming scenarios — within years, not generations.
17,000 km²
Patagonian Ice Fields
Patagonia hosts the largest temperate ice fields outside the polar regions — the last major Andean glaciers expected to persist through the century.

A century of retreat

The Disappearing Ice

What was permanent landscape for the Inca has become ephemeral in a single human lifetime.

1900
Glaciers define the Andean skyline
The tropical Andes hold roughly 2,500 km² of ice. Rivers flowing from glaciers are reliable year-round. Indigenous communities and colonial cities alike depend on them.
1930s
First scientific measurements
Systematic glacier monitoring begins in Peru and Bolivia. Scientists note marginal retreat but attribute it to natural variability.
Estimated ice cover: ~2,300 km²
1970s
Acceleration begins
Andean glaciers begin retreating at measurably faster rates. Studies link the change to rising temperatures from industrial emissions. Chacaltaya ski resort in Bolivia opens — at 5,395 m, the world's highest.
1998
El Niño accelerates warming
An exceptionally strong El Niño event causes sudden, dramatic losses. Quelccaya Ice Cap in Peru — the world's largest tropical ice mass — begins retreating at double its prior rate.
Quelccaya retreat: 50 m/year
2009
Chacaltaya is gone
Bolivia's Chacaltaya glacier — once a ski destination — disappears entirely. What took millennia to form vanished in decades. It had been projected to survive until 2015; it melted six years early.
2023
Critical threshold
Peruvian glaciers now cover less than half their 1950s extent. Lima — a city of 10 million in a desert — enters its first severe glacially-linked water crisis.
Ice lost since 1950: > 50%

Where the ice remains

Glacial Regions of the Andes

From tropical snowfields in Colombia to sub-polar ice fields in Patagonia, the Andean cryosphere spans 66 degrees of latitude. Each region faces a different timeline of loss.

Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta
Colombia — 10°N
The world's most northerly tropical glacier. Currently 16 km² — down from 45 km² in 1850. Projected to vanish by 2040.
16 km² remaining
Cordillera Blanca
Peru — 9°S
The world's largest concentration of tropical glaciers. Feeds rivers sustaining 30 cities. Has lost 35% of area since 1970.
~527 km² remaining
Quelccaya Ice Cap
Peru — 14°S
The world's largest tropical ice cap and a key climate archive. Ice cores here contain 1,500 years of climate data. Retreating at 50+ m/year.
~44 km² remaining
Northern Patagonian Ice Field
Chile — 47°S
4,200 km² of continuous ice. Feeds spectacular fjords and outlet glaciers including the San Rafael Glacier. Losing mass but projected to survive longest.
4,200 km² remaining
Perito Moreno
Argentina — 50°S
The rare exception: one of very few glaciers in the world currently in equilibrium, neither advancing nor retreating. A 250 km² wall of blue ice, 60 m tall at its face.
250 km² — stable

What comes next

After the Ice

When Andean glaciers disappear, they do not simply vanish — they are replaced by a void. Seasonal rivers that once ran reliably dry up in the months of maximum demand. Ecosystems adapted to cold, stable temperatures collapse.

But new glacial lakes form in the hollows left behind. Some are spectacular. Some are dangerous — glacial lake outburst floods, or GLOFs, can devastate valleys in hours.

The question is not whether the glaciers will go. The question is what societies and ecosystems will replace them — and whether they will be ready.

"The glaciers are the water towers of the Andes. When they are gone, the mountains will still be there. But the mountains will be thirsty." — Lonnie Thompson, glaciologist, Ohio State University
