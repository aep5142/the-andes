02 — Civilization

The people of the high peaks

The Inca and the Mountains That Were Gods

For the Inca, the mountains were not a backdrop — they were deities. Every major peak was an Apu, a divine spirit controlling rain, frost, harvests, and fate. An entire empire was built in vertical relationship with the Andes.

The Mountain Gods — Apus
Every major peak was a living deity — the higher, the more powerful. The most sacred Inca ritual, the capacocha, brought offerings to the highest reachable summits. Mummies found at 6,739 m on Llullaillaco remain the highest archaeological site on Earth.
6,739 m — Llullaillaco capacocha
Qhapaq Ñan — The Royal Road
40,000 km of roads across the Andes, more extensive than Rome's network at its peak. Stone-paved, with suspension bridges over canyons and relay runners (chasquis) carrying messages 240 km a day. A UNESCO World Heritage site crossing six nations.
40,000 km of roads
Andenes — Terrace Agriculture
The Inca transformed vertical mountainsides into productive farmland through andenes — stone terraces with built-in irrigation, frost protection, and soil management. Maize below, potatoes and quinoa above. Over one million hectares of terracing still stand across the Andes.
1,000,000+ hectares terraced
Machu Picchu — City in the Clouds
Built at 2,430 m on a ridge above the Urubamba River — simultaneously a royal estate, agricultural center, and astronomical observatory. Its alignments with solstice sunrises and mountain peaks show an empire that read the cosmos through the mountains.
Built c. 1450 CE at 2,430 m
Empire and Enduring Legacy
At its peak, the Inca Empire stretched 4,000 km along the Andes with a population of 12 million. The Spanish conquest of 1532 dismantled the empire, but not its people. Today ~10 million speak Quechua. Communities still hold ceremonies on mountain summits, still call the peaks Apus.
~10 million Quechua speakers today

05 — Water

Water from the sacred mountains

The Ice That Feeds a Continent

The Inca understood what hydrologists confirm today: the mountains are the source. Their snowfields feed the rivers that made coastal civilizations possible — the lifeblood flowing from heights the Inca called sacred.

~1,800 glaciers
Tropical glaciers
Over 99% of all tropical glaciers on Earth are in the Andes. The Inca built their irrigation systems directly from glacial meltwater flowing off the mountains.
30%
Of South America's freshwater
Andean glaciers supply nearly a third of the continent's freshwater — sustaining the coastal cities and ancient irrigation systems of the Pacific lowlands.
17,200 km²
Southern Patagonian Ice Field
The largest temperate ice mass in the Southern Hemisphere. The Patagonian glaciers were the Inca's southern boundary — the cold wilderness beyond which civilization ended.
40%
Volume lost since 1980
The sacred mountains are retreating. Tropical Andean glaciers have lost 40% of their area in 40 years, threatening the freshwater supply the Inca built cities to receive.

07 — Astronomy

Reading the sky above the Andes

From Inca Astronomers to Radio Telescopes

The Inca tracked the Pleiades for agricultural calendars, identified dark constellations in the Milky Way's dust lanes, and aligned cities to solstice positions. The Atacama Plateau that they knew as sacred high ground is now home to the most powerful astronomical instruments ever built.

ALMA — 5,058 m
The Atacama Large Millimeter Array — 66 high-precision antennas on the Chajnantor Plateau. The most powerful radio telescope array on Earth, studying the cold universe: star and planet birthplaces, the building blocks of galaxies.
Why the Atacama
Less than 1 mm of water vapor above. 330+ cloudless nights per year. At 5,000 m, the telescope works above half the atmosphere. No light pollution for hundreds of kilometers. The same qualities that made this plateau sacred to the Inca make it priceless to science.
Planets Forming in Real Time
In 2014, ALMA captured the first images of planets forming around a young star (HL Tauri) — an observation that overturned existing models of solar system formation. Made from a plateau the Inca had watched the sky from for 5,000 years.
Observatory
Very Large Telescope (VLT)
Four 8.2-metre telescopes on Cerro Paranal, Chile, at 2,635 m. It has directly imaged an exoplanet and taken some of the deepest images of the universe ever captured.
Observatory
Cerro Tololo Inter-American
Located at 2,200 m in the Chilean Andes. Home to the Dark Energy Survey — mapping 300 million galaxies to understand the force accelerating the expansion of the universe.
Future
Extremely Large Telescope
Under construction on Cerro Armazones, Chile, at 3,060 m. When complete in 2028, its 39-metre mirror will be the largest optical telescope ever built — perched in the Andes, looking at the edge of time.
