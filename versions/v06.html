Fifteen thousand years of human civilization, carved into the highest inhabited mountains on Earth. The Andes were not a barrier. They were a home.

13,000 BCE — Present

A chronology

The Civilizations of the High Andes

Human beings reached the Andes shortly after arriving in South America — and never left. Each civilization that rose here solved the same fundamental challenge: how to live and thrive at altitudes that crush outsiders.

13,000 BCE
Paleoindian
First Andean Peoples
Southern Andes, Patagonia & Altiplano
Hunter-gatherers reach the Andes, following herds of native camelids (ancestors of the llama and alpaca) onto the high grasslands. Cave paintings at Cueva de las Manos, Argentina document this presence.
Evidence of settlement at 4,500 m altitude — earliest known high-altitude habitation on Earth
3,000 BCE
Preceramic
Norte Chico / Caral
Coastal Peru — Andean foothills
The oldest known complex civilization in the Americas. Cities with monumental architecture, irrigation agriculture, and evidence of quipu (knotted cord recording) — all without pottery. Caral-Supe was a city of 20,000 people when Egypt was building its first pyramids.
Contemporary with Ancient Egypt. Over 25 cities in a river valley network.
900 BCE
Early Horizon
Chavín
Northern Highlands, Peru — 3,177 m
The first pan-Andean cultural horizon — a religious movement spreading its distinctive art style and iconography across hundreds of miles. Chavín de Huántar, at 3,177 m, was a pilgrimage site. Its art features the Staff God, an image that persisted in Andean religion for 3,000 years.
First known pan-Andean religion. The feathered jaguar staff god persists through the Inca.
100 BCE
Early Intermediate
Nazca & Moche
Southern & Northern Peru
Two parallel civilizations on opposite coasts and altitudes. The Nazca carved the famous geoglyphs — lines and figures visible only from the air — into the desert. The Moche created extraordinary ceramic portraiture and built the Huaca del Sol pyramid from 140 million bricks.
Nazca Lines: 800 km of lines across 80 km² of desert. Purpose still debated.
500 CE
Middle Horizon
Tiwanaku
Lake Titicaca, Bolivia — 3,850 m
The highest major civilization in history, built on the shore of Lake Titicaca at 3,850 m above sea level. At its height it fed 400,000 people using raised field agriculture on seasonally flooded land. The Gateway of the Sun, carved from a single 44-ton stone, still stands.
Population: 400,000. Altitude record for a major civilization.
1100 CE
Late Intermediate
Chimú & Wari
Coastal & Highland Peru
The Chimú empire built Chan Chan, the largest pre-Columbian city in South America and the largest adobe city in the world. The Wari pioneered road systems and administrative techniques later adopted — and expanded — by the Inca.
Chan Chan: 20 km² of walled citadels — a UNESCO World Heritage Site today.
1438 CE
Late Horizon
Tawantinsuyu — The Inca Empire
Cusco, Peru outward to 7 nations
The largest empire in pre-Columbian America and the largest empire in the Southern Hemisphere ever recorded. At its peak it stretched 4,000 km along the Andes — from modern Colombia to Chile. It was administered through a network of 40,000 km of roads, relay runners, and quipu accounting systems.
40,000 km of road. 10–12 million subjects. No written language — yet a functioning empire.
1532 CE
Colonial era
Spanish Conquest
Cajamarca, Peru
Francisco Pizarro captures the Inca emperor Atahualpa with 168 soldiers against tens of thousands. It is one of the most consequential and devastating moments in world history. Within decades, 90% of the indigenous population is dead from violence and disease.
The Inca offered a room full of gold as ransom. Spain took the gold and executed Atahualpa anyway.

The Inca Empire in numbers

Tawantinsuyu — The Four Corners of the World

The Inca built the last and largest indigenous empire in the Americas in less than a century — without the wheel, without iron, and without a written language.

40,000
Kilometers of road
Two parallel highways ran the length of the empire — one coastal, one Andean. Suspension bridges crossed gorges. Relay runners (chasquis) could carry messages 2,400 km in just 5 days.
200+
Varieties of potato developed
The Inca (and earlier Andean civilizations) domesticated the potato, which now feeds more of humanity than any other crop outside grain. They also developed freeze-drying — chuño potato — thousands of years before modern food preservation.
3,400
Meters — Cusco, the capital
The imperial capital Cusco sat at 3,400 m, laid out in the shape of a puma. Its stone architecture — massive, earthquake-resistant, fitted without mortar — has outlasted every colonial building built on top of it.
2,430
Meters — Machu Picchu
Built around 1450 CE, Machu Picchu is thought to be an Inca royal estate. It was abandoned — not destroyed. The Spanish never found it. Its terraced agricultural fields and precisely fitted stone survive intact after nearly 600 years.

Language of the mountains

Quechua: The Living Language of the Inca

Quechua, the language of the Inca Empire, is still spoken by 8–10 million people across the Andes. It is an official language in Peru, Ecuador, and Bolivia. Some of its words have entered English.

Pacha
Earth / Time / World
A single word holds earth, time, and the universe simultaneously. The Andean concept of reality is not separated by category.
Apu
Mountain spirit / Deity
Each major Andean peak is an Apu — a living divine being. Offerings are still left at mountain passes throughout Peru and Bolivia.
Ayni
Sacred reciprocity
The principle that governs all Andean social life: reciprocity between people, between people and the earth, between the living and the dead.
Quipu
Knotted cord record
Became "quipu" in English. The Inca recorded census data, stories, and accounts in knotted cords. Scholars are still decoding what the knots say.
Condor
Andean condor bird
From Quechua kuntur. With a wingspan of 3.3 m, the condor was sacred — the link between the earthly world and the heavens. Now an endangered species.
Llama
The camelid
Entered Spanish and English directly from Quechua llama. Domesticated 6,000 years ago from the wild vicuña, the llama was the only large pack animal indigenous to the Americas.

What remains

The Andes Still Remembers

The colonial period lasted 300 years and dismantled nearly everything visible. But it could not dismantle what people carried in their bodies: language, agricultural knowledge, social structures, spiritual practices, music.

Today, Andean civilizations are not merely historical. Quechua is spoken by 10 million people. Inca agricultural terraces — abandoned after conquest — are being restored. The ceremonies continue. The offerings are still left on mountain passes at 4,000 meters.

The mountains endured. The people endured. Fifteen thousand years of accumulated knowledge — how to grow crops at altitude, how to manage water across vast distances, how to build in earthquake country — are still here. And the world is finally beginning to pay attention.

"They did not leave ruins. They left a living world. Everything the Inca built was designed to be inhabited, maintained, grown — not preserved. The real legacy of the Andes is not stone. It is the people still here." — Anthropologist, Universidad Nacional de San Antonio Abad del Cusco
