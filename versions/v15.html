Geography

The Spine of South America

The Andes run the entire western edge of the continent — from the Caribbean coast of Venezuela to the subpolar tip of Tierra del Fuego. A near-vertical wall, hugging the Pacific, that defines a continent's shape.

Elevation

The Mightiest Peaks Outside Asia

Five of the six highest peaks outside the Himalayas rise from the Andes. An unbroken wall of summits above 6,000 m runs from Ecuador to Patagonia — a scale of elevation found nowhere outside Asia.

"Only the Himalayas surpass what the Andes has quietly built over millions of years."

The Andes rise because the Nazca oceanic plate dives beneath South America at roughly 70 millimetres per year — one of Earth's fastest subduction rates. As the Pacific plate descends, it compresses and lifts the continental crust above. The result: 70 km of crustal thickness beneath the Andes, nearly double the global average, and a mountain chain that has been growing for 25 million years.

Civilization

The Mountains That Were Gods

For the Inca, every major peak was an Apu — a divine spirit controlling rain, frost, harvests, and human fate. The higher the mountain, the greater its power. An entire empire was built in direct vertical relationship with the Andes.

Machu Picchu, built at 2,430 m on a ridge above the Urubamba River around 1450 CE, was simultaneously a royal estate, an agricultural center, and an astronomical observatory — its walls aligned to solstice sunrises and the positions of sacred mountain peaks.

The most sacred Inca ritual, the capacocha, involved offerings — sometimes human — carried to the highest accessible summits. Mummies discovered at 6,739 m on Llullaillaco in Argentina are the highest archaeological finds on Earth: three children, perfectly preserved by the cold, placed there in the early 1500s for the mountain's gods.

"The Inca did not conquer the Andes. They were shaped by them — a civilization that grew from the mountains' logic of altitude, water, and sky."

Today, roughly ten million people speak Quechua, the Inca language, across Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, Colombia, and Argentina. Andean communities still hold ceremonies on mountain peaks, still call the mountains Apus, still manage terrace systems engineered five hundred years ago. The empire fell. The relationship with the mountains did not.

Hydrology

The Ice That Feeds a Continent

The Andes hold more than 99% of all tropical glaciers on Earth and the Southern Hemisphere's second-largest ice mass outside Antarctica. Their meltwater is the foundation of life across the entire Pacific coast.

Astronomy

Where Science Meets the Universe

The Atacama Plateau — 4,000 to 5,000 metres above sea level, at the Andean western escarpment — offers conditions for astronomy unmatched anywhere on Earth. Less than one millimetre of precipitable water vapor in the atmosphere. More than 330 cloudless nights per year. Air so thin and dry that the universe comes into sharp focus.

The Inca already knew this. They tracked the Pleiades for agricultural calendars, identified dark constellations formed by the Milky Way's dust lanes — not by stars, but by the absences between them — and aligned their cities and temples to solstice and equinox positions with extraordinary precision. The same plateau where ALMA now stands was already a place where humans looked up and tried to understand the cosmos.

"Astronomers call the Atacama the closest thing on Earth to outer space."

The Atacama Large Millimeter Array — 66 high-precision antennas on the Chajnantor Plateau at 5,058 m — is the most powerful radio telescope array ever built. In 2014, it captured the first detailed images of a protoplanetary disk around a young star, showing planets forming in real time. A single observation that overturned decades of models about how solar systems develop.
